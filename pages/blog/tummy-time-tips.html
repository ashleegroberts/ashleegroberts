Tummy Time From Day One —
Why It Matters More Than You Think

By Ashlee Roberts 2025 6 min read

If there is one thing most new parents hear but don't really act on right away, it's tummy time. It sounds simple — put the baby on their stomach, wait a few minutes, done. But the reason pediatricians, occupational therapists, and developmental specialists talk about it so consistently is that tummy time is not just one activity. It is the foundation every single major motor milestone is built on. Rolling, sitting, crawling, standing, walking — all of it starts here.

The other thing worth knowing: you can start on day one. You don't have to wait until your baby seems "ready." In fact, the earlier you start, the easier it gets — for both of you.

What tummy time is actually doing

When a baby is placed on their stomach, the position activates the extensor muscles along their back, neck, and shoulders. These are the muscles that work against gravity — and gravity is the first physical challenge your baby faces outside the womb. Without regular practice in this position, those muscles don't develop the strength and endurance they need to hit milestones on a healthy timeline.

There is also a structural benefit. Because babies spend so much time on their backs — in car seats, bouncy seats, on their backs to sleep, which is absolutely right for safe sleep — the back of the skull is under consistent pressure. Tummy time takes that pressure off and helps prevent positional plagiocephaly, commonly called flat head syndrome.

And then there is the shoulder girdle. The strength built in the shoulders and upper arms during tummy time is directly used later for sitting independently, for crawling with stability, and eventually for the fine motor skills in the hands that come from having a strong proximal base. Strong shoulders make for capable hands.

The rule that never changes: Tummy time is only ever for awake, supervised babies. Back to sleep, tummy to play — always, without exception. This is non-negotiable.

Why most parents start too late

A lot of parents wait until their baby seems to "have enough neck control" before starting tummy time. This is the most common misconception. Tummy time is what builds neck control — it is the training ground, not the graduation ceremony. Waiting until baby can already hold their head up means you have already missed weeks of the window where this practice is most impactful.

The second reason parents delay is that babies often cry during tummy time, especially in the early weeks. This gets interpreted as "my baby hates it" or "my baby isn't ready." What is actually happening is that they are working muscles they have never used before, in a position that requires real effort. It is hard. That is the point. Short, frequent sessions are the answer — not waiting until it stops being hard.

How to do tummy time from day one

The best starting point is your own chest, not the floor. Skin-to-skin with baby chest-to-chest, their head turned to one side, this counts as tummy time. Your body heat, your heartbeat, and your face directly in their line of sight make it the most comfortable version of the position. Most newborns tolerate this much more easily than being placed on a flat surface.

When you move to the floor, timing matters. Choose a window when baby is awake and alert — not hungry, not right after a feed, not sleepy. An alert baby is a participating baby.

Here is what a realistic tummy time progression looks like through the first three months:

  • Week 1 to 2: 1 to 2 minutes per session, 2 to 3 times a day on your chest or a firm flat surface. Just getting used to the position.
  • Weeks 3 to 4: Start transitioning to the floor more consistently. 3 to 5 minutes per session. Place a high-contrast card or your face directly in front of them — give them something worth lifting their head for.
  • Month 2: Sessions can stretch to 5 to 10 minutes. Baby will start pushing up more deliberately, turning their head side to side, and showing more curiosity about their surroundings from this position.
  • Month 3: Build toward 30 or more total minutes throughout the day. This does not have to be one long session — it can be 5 or 6 shorter ones. Baby should be pushing up onto forearms consistently by the end of this month.

What to do when baby hates it

Get on the floor with them. Seriously, this is the single most effective thing you can do. When baby sees your face at their level, the motivation to lift their head dramatically increases. Your presence changes the experience from "hard and uncomfortable" to "hard and worth it."

A rolled towel or small receiving blanket placed under their chest, just below their armpits, takes some weight off and makes the position easier to tolerate while they build strength. Remove it gradually as they get stronger.

If baby cries persistently, pick them up, soothe them, and try again a little later. Short and frequent is always better than long and miserable. Five two-minute sessions beat one ten-minute battle every time.

A tummy time mirror placed at their eye level is one of the best tools available. Babies will hold their head up significantly longer when they are looking at a face — even their own reflection. An unbreakable acrylic mirror propped in front of them turns a frustrating session into a genuinely engaging one.

Signs tummy time is working

By the end of month one, you should see baby starting to turn their head from side to side when placed prone. By month two, they will begin to lift their head briefly and hold it for a second or two. By month three, they should be pushing up onto their forearms and holding their head at a 45-degree angle or higher.

If you are hitting these checkpoints consistently, tummy time is doing its job. If you feel like your baby is significantly behind on head control, mention it to your pediatrician — there is a range of normal, and your doctor can tell you whether any additional support is needed.

The bottom line

Tummy time is not a box to check. It is the foundation of your baby's entire first year of physical development, and the earlier and more consistently you do it, the easier every milestone that follows becomes. Start today, even if your baby is already a few weeks old. Short sessions, your face at their level, and patience — that is genuinely all it takes.
